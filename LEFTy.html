LEFTy

Language Efficient Text portray

Author Profiling






In times of deep learning's massive expansion new applications for it aren't surprising, but they are welcome. Progress in NLP however has been slow and unconvincing to some researchers. Recent months, however, have shown unpredentent progress in the field using using transfer learning based on a langauge model trained on massive amounts of data. Current trend is to use transformers, however training and infering requires more computational power and additional costs.

Transfer learning has been a concept that was thought not to be applicable for NLP since great results were never achieved. However, ELMo and ULMFiT changed that in early 2018 with new SOTA results across many different tasks. Big labs like OpenAI and Google then followed it up with GPT (2) and BERT which use transformers to train different kinds of LM's on massive and diversive text corpora.

Results achieved using LEFTy (based on fine tuning techniques outlined by Howard and Ruder in their ULMFiT paper) are near the SOTA for author profiling.

LEFTy


A language model was trained on Spanish Wikipedia articles using both and an LSTM and a QRNN model. More on how the language model was trained can be found here.

Training and testing sets used to train this project were the PAN'17 and SpanText corpora. Project's objective was specifically creating a text profiler for the Spanish language which is why those corpora were used.

The fine tuning techniques for both the LM and the classifier models are taken from the ULMFiT paper linked above. The process and full results are presented in my undergrad thesis on this subject (in Spanish).

Demo


Simple demo is shown here as a proof of concept. The model used in this demo works best with formal texts of medium length. Cleaning process for this demo isn't exhaustive so in order for it to work best, avoid emojis, URLs, etc. Once you've input the text you'll get a an age and gender prediction. The model used for these formal texts has an accuracy of 71% of predicting both traits correctly.
